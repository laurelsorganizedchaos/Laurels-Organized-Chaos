Organized Chaos: Brains, Burnout & Being Human

Written by Laurel · For the days when “existing” is the win

I wish more people understood that “tired” doesn’t always mean “I stayed up too late.” Sometimes tired means my brain has been running on survival mode for years and I’m trying to do normal life on a nervous system that never got the memo we’re safe now.

When I say “organized chaos,” I’m not being cute. My brain really is both. I’m the girl with big ideas and ten tabs open in my head, who also forgets what she walked into the room for and needs three reminders to drink water.

If your days swing between hyper-fixated productivity and “I can’t move from this spot,” hi. You’re not broken — you’re human, and your brain is probably carrying a lot.

When your brain doesn’t match the world’s expectations

The world is built for people who can think in straight lines. Wake up, do the things, check the boxes, repeat. Easy.

But some of us have:

  • Brains that drift, loop, or hyper-focus and then crash.
  • Emotions that show up like weather — sudden, intense, confusing.
  • Histories that taught us to scan for danger instead of relax.
  • Energy levels that don’t care what’s on the calendar.

You start to think, “What’s wrong with me?” Why can’t I keep up? Why can’t I just do the simple things everyone else does?

Here’s the thing: there is nothing wrong with you for struggling. Your brain is not a machine that failed quality control. It’s a living, adapting, exhausted organ that’s been trying very hard to keep you alive.

Burnout isn’t just being busy

Burnout gets talked about like it’s just too many tasks or too much work. But the burnout I know isn’t just a long to-do list — it’s a long everything.

It’s:

  • Carrying old stress in a body that never got to exhale.
  • Showing up for everyone else and putting yourself dead last.
  • Masking how overwhelmed you are so no one worries or judges.
  • Never really feeling “off duty,” even when you’re lying down.

One day it all just… catches up. The dishes feel impossible. Texts go unanswered. You stare at a simple task and your brain says, “Nope. We’re done.”

That isn’t laziness. That’s a body and mind that hit their limit.

Organization that doesn’t require pretending you’re a robot

A lot of “productivity advice” is made for people who are already functioning pretty well. Wake up at 5. Make a 27-step morning routine. Bullet journal your feelings. Just “be disciplined.”

Cute for them. Not for all of us.

If your brain is already overloaded, adding a complicated system on top of it is just another way to fail.

Realistic organization for a chaotic brain looks more like:

  • Lowering the bar. Instead of “fix my whole life,” it’s “what’s the one thing that would make today feel 5% less heavy?”
  • Externalizing your brain. Write things down, use alarms, post-it notes, dumb systems. Your brain doesn’t have to hold everything alone.
  • Planning for low-energy days. Have a “bare minimum” version of your routine that counts.
  • Letting imperfect still count. Two minutes of tidying. One email sent. One glass of water. It all counts.

“Organized” doesn’t have to mean sterile, rigid, or aesthetic. It can mean “I have just enough structure that I don’t completely drown when life gets loud.”

The shame spiral: “Everyone else can do this, why can’t I?”

One of the hardest parts of burnout + brain chaos is the shame. You compare yourself to people who seem to breeze through life and think, “If I were stronger, I’d be fine.”

But you don’t know their nervous system. You don’t know their history, their support, their wiring. You only know that you are working very, very hard — even when it looks like you’re “doing nothing.”

Your internal effort doesn’t always match what the outside world can see. Just getting up, feeding yourself, caring for kids, going to appointments, studying, trying again — that is work.

You are not behind. You are not failing at “being a person.” You are moving at the speed your body/brain can handle right now.

Making room for your whole self

Organized chaos, to me, means building a life that has room for all of you:

  • The version that gets stuff done and feels powerful.
  • The version that melts down and needs to cry in the shower.
  • The version that is silly, witchy, creative, and a little unhinged.
  • The version that needs naps, meds, therapy, and time.

You don’t have to pick one. You’re allowed to be a mix.

Some days, “organized” will mean you tackled five things. Other days, it will mean you didn’t quit on yourself even when your brain was screaming for escape.

What if you believed existing was enough today?

I’m not here to tell you to stay stuck. I believe in growth, in healing, in slowly making your life kinder to live inside of.

But I also believe we underestimate how much strength it takes to simply stay when everything feels heavy.

So if today:

  • You woke up and you’re still here.
  • You fed yourself something, even if it wasn’t “healthy.”
  • You took your meds or at least thought about it.
  • You showed up for someone you love, even a little.

That matters. That counts.

Your brain, your burnout, your messy beautiful humanness — it’s all part of the story, not a disqualification from it.

You don’t have to become a different person to deserve a better life. You get to build one as the you that exists right now: chaotic, sensitive, tired, trying — and still worthy.
