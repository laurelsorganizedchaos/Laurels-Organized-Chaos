Organized Chaos: Brains, Burnout & Being Human

Written by Laurel

Let me be very clear about something:

Trying to stay organized when you have four kids, a full-time school load, an online business, a marriage, and a brain that refuses to act “normal” is… a whole-ass lifestyle.

People talk about “balancing everything” like it’s some soft, aesthetic, Pinterest moment. Meanwhile, I’m over here managing:

– Three kids ages three and under who live with me
– A soon-to-be 13-year-old daughter who lives with my mom
– Being a wife
– Being a full-time student
– Starting an online business
– Managing my mental health

Let me tell you — this is not balance. This is survival with eyeliner.

And after trying every organizational method, planner, app, calendar, and system made for neurotypical humans, I finally had to face the truth:

Those systems weren’t built for me.

Letting Go of the “Perfect Routine” Fantasy

I used to chase the idea of a perfect routine.

Wake up at the same time. Have the same morning schedule. Color-coded charts. Time blocks. Motivational quotes taped to the wall, like they were going to magically cure my ADHD.

Instead, here’s what actually happened:

– I’d spend hours building routines.
– I’d try to follow them for a day or two.
– Real life would punch through the schedule.
– I’d feel like a failure.
– I’d throw the whole thing away and start over again.

A “perfect routine” makes my life feel like Groundhog Day — and not in the cute Bill Murray way. More like the “I am slowly losing my sanity” way.

So I dropped the idea of perfect routines. Completely.

Honestly? It was one of the best things I’ve ever done for my mental health.

What Actually Works for My Brain

Let’s talk about planners.

Digital planners? Notification reminders? Calendar apps? To-do apps?

No. Absolutely not.

I open my phone with the intention of checking my planner and next thing I know I’m deep into Facebook reels, TikTok chaos, or researching something totally random I do not need at 9 a.m.

My ADHD brain cannot be trusted with a smartphone.

Physical planners saved my life. Actual notebooks. Ones I can hold. Ones I can flip through. Ones I have to physically write in — because writing keeps my brain present instead of floating into the void.

But also… because I am who I am…

I need many notebooks.

Eventually a notebook “loses its vibe,” or the subject no longer feels right, or the energy gets chaotic. The ADHD gremlin in my brain decides, “I can’t use this anymore,” and suddenly I need a fresh one.

Is it logical? Not really. Does it work? Surprisingly, yes.

My Day-to-Day: Barely Organized, But Still Working

In my house, only three things happen at a set time every single day:

– Lunch at 12 pm
– Dinner between 5–6 pm
– Bedtime between 8–9 pm

Everything else? We take it day by day.

My morning routine looks something like this:

1. Wake up when the kids wake up.
2. Make breakfast.
3. Feed them.
4. Straighten up the house.
5. Write out my to-do list for the day.

Not a fancy to-do list. Not a prioritized, color-coded, aesthetic list.

More like: whatever I remember, in whatever order it pops into my head, written as fast as possible before the thought evaporates.

Because honestly? I have the memory of Drew Barrymore in 50 First Dates mixed with Dory from Finding Nemo. If I don’t write it down immediately, it is gone.

After I write the list, I also write everything I’ve already done that morning — because it helps me see I’ve actually accomplished things and keeps me motivated.

I tape the list to the fridge, and throughout the day I:

– Check things off.
– Add new tasks.
– Write down stuff I did that wasn’t even on the list (because that still counts).

Some days I finish everything. Some days I don’t.

Both days are okay.

Building Without Burning Myself Out

The biggest thing I’ve learned is this:

I don’t improve my life by overhauling everything at once. I improve it by adding one small thing at a time — when I’m ready.

Once I feel confident keeping up with the basics of house stuff and life, then I start adding things like:

– Working out
– Hobbies
– Business tasks
– Self-care time
– Extra study sessions

Not all at once. Not in a perfect routine. Not in a rigid way that will burn me out in a week.

But slowly. Intentionally. Like stacking stones instead of juggling knives.

I’ve realized that setting my intention matters more than setting the perfect system.

I know what I want to be. I know where I want to go. And I know I’m capable of getting there — as long as I let myself do it in a way that works for my brain.

This Is What “Organized Chaos” Really Means

Organized Chaos, for me, doesn’t mean perfection. It doesn’t mean rigid routines. It doesn’t mean forcing myself into systems built for someone else’s mind and life.

It means taking life day by day, being honest about who I am, and building systems that match my real energy — not the imaginary version of myself I used to perform.

It looks like scribbled lists on the fridge. Too many half-used notebooks. Flexible days anchored by a few solid routines. Small steps toward big goals. Messy progress that still counts.

Somehow, that messy, scribbled, ADHD-friendly system?

It works. Not because it’s perfect — but because it’s mine.
