A Chaotically Blessed Thanksgiving (Hold the Family Drama)

When my husband isn’t welcome, neither am I or our kids. So instead of begging for a seat at someone else’s table, we built our own — messy, loud, and full of real love.

This is the story of an early-morning Thanksgiving that started with baby bottles and toddlers, and turned into a reminder that “family” is the people who actually show up with respect, not the ones who demand access while talking shit.

Most of my family has decided they hate my husband. Not because they’ve actually spent time with him, but because it’s easier to believe rumors than to admit they might have been wrong about people in their own bloodline. At some point I decided: if my husband isn’t welcome, then neither am I, and absolutely neither are our kids.

So this year, instead of forcing myself into a house full of fake smiles and side-eye, I had Thanksgiving early with the people who didn’t sign up for the drama. The ones who treat Tyler with basic human respect. The ones who don’t expect me to split myself in half to make everyone else comfortable.

The morning started at 7:15 a.m. when Nash and Hudson woke me up (because of course they did). I got up, made Nash her bottle, wrangled Hudson, and we slid straight into holiday chaos: feeding, cleaning, trying to time naps, and prepping food while making sure nobody launched themselves off the furniture.

It wasn’t peaceful. It wasn’t aesthetic. There were crumbs, meltdowns, and the constant background noise of “Mom? Mom. Mooooom.” But it was ours.

Choosing Our Own Table

At some point you have to stop chasing people who only love the version of you they made up in their heads. This year, I chose not to sit at a table where my husband is treated like a problem to be tolerated. I chose to build a smaller table with fewer people and more actual safety.

Our Thanksgiving wasn’t perfect. The turkey wasn’t carved in some cinematic way. The kids were picky about the food. We were tired. But there was laughter, there were inside jokes, there were real hugs instead of stiff side-hugs with resentment tucked underneath.

Family Isn’t Always Blood

The older I get, the more I realize that “family” is a very specific kind of spell. It’s not formed by DNA; it’s formed by the people who defend you when you’re not in the room, who accept your partner, who love your kids, and who don’t ask you to shrink.

I’m done apologizing for choosing my husband and my kids over people who refuse to treat us like a unit. I’m done chasing approval I will never get. I’m allowed to protect my peace, even on holidays that are supposed to be “for family.”

My Little Gratitude List

  • For a husband who loves me, even when I’m exhausted and overstimulated.
  • For tiny humans who drive me insane and also keep me going.
  • For friends and relatives who didn’t join the drama and just showed up with love.
  • For the reminder that my home can be a safe place, even if certain people never walk through the door again.

This Thanksgiving wasn’t about pretending everything is fine. It was about admitting that some things are broken, choosing not to keep cutting myself on the pieces, and building something better with the people who actually want to be here.

If you spent a holiday away from “family” this year to protect your sanity, you’re not selfish. You’re healing.
