Motherhood Without the Mask

Written by Laurel

Let’s start with this so there’s no confusion: I love my kids more than anything in this world. I would do absolutely anything for them.

And also… there are days where, if I’m being completely honest, I joke that I’d like to drop them off at the fire department and drive away for a few hours.

Not because I don’t love them. Not because I’m a bad mom. But because motherhood is intense, relentless, overstimulating, and some days it feels like my body, brain, and nervous system are all screaming at once.

The good news? Since letting go of unrealistic expectations, dropping the lies about what a “good mom” is supposed to look like, and basically relearning parenting from scratch every day — those fire-station-joke days are happening a lot less.

Letting Go of the “Good Mom” Script

For a long time, I was carrying around this invisible script about what being a “good mom” meant.

A good mom always has a perfect routine. A good mom plays with her kids constantly. A good mom keeps them busy, stimulated, engaged, learning, smiling 24/7. A good mom doesn’t get touched out, burned out, or secretly fantasize about silence.

You know what that script did?

It didn’t make me a better mom. It just made me exhausted, guilty, resentful, and constantly feeling like I was failing at a role I was literally giving my whole life to.

So I started asking myself:

What if “good mom” doesn’t mean perfect, self-sacrificing, always-on mom? What if “good mom” just means human mom who loves her kids and is trying?

Kids Don’t Need a Perfect Routine

Do kids need structure? Yes. Do they need some predictability? Yes. Do they need every minute of every day planned out with developmental activities, themed snacks, and scheduled joy?

Absolutely not.

I used to think that if I didn’t have a Pinterest-worthy routine, I was failing them. But a “perfect routine” isn’t real life. Life with kids is fluid. Someone wakes up sick. Someone doesn’t sleep. Someone spills something. Someone melts down. Sometimes that someone is you.

My kids have anchors — meals, bedtime, a general flow — but I stopped trying to choreograph every moment. The more I relaxed, the more they did too.

You Don’t Have to Be Their Entertainment System

One of the biggest lies I had to unlearn:

“A good mom spends every second playing with her kids.”

No. Actually, that’s not healthy — for them or for you.

Kids need to learn how to play by themselves. They need to build independence, imagination, and the ability to entertain themselves without a grown-up constantly directing the script.

I still do arts and crafts with them. I still read books. I still get on the floor and play. But I also say:

“Okay, now it’s solo play time.” “Mommy’s doing dishes / schoolwork / sitting for a second.” “You figure out what game you want to play.”

That doesn’t make me a bad mom. It makes me a human mom with human limits — and it makes them kids who know how to exist without constant entertainment.

They’re Allowed to Be Bored

Another thing I had to rewire in my brain:

My kids do not have to be doing something productive, educational, or Instagram-cute every second of the day.

They are allowed to be bored. They are allowed to chill. They are allowed to flop on the couch and just exist.

Learning how to be bored is actually important. It teaches them:

– How to sit with themselves
– How to come up with ideas on their own
– How to rest without guilt
– That life doesn’t always have to be loud and fast to be okay

I still look up crafts. I still plan fun things. I still try to be intentional. But I’ve stopped treating “constant stimulation” like a requirement of motherhood.

One-Size-Fits-All Parenting Is a Lie

I have four kids. They share DNA, a mom, a dad, a family, and some overlapping chaos… but personality-wise?

Completely different humans.

Some are more sensitive. Some are more stubborn. Some need more structure. Some need more freedom. Some need more physical closeness. Some need more space.

There is no “one-size-fits-all” parenting method that works on all of them. I’ve had to accept that:

– What works for one kid might absolutely flop for another.
– Discipline, comfort, and communication all look different from child to child.
– Fair doesn’t always mean “exactly the same.”

Parenting has to be tailored. Adjusted. Customized. Not just to each kid — but also to who you are as a parent.

Taking the Mask Off, For Them and For Me

Motherhood without the mask, for me, means:

– Admitting it’s hard without adding “but I shouldn’t complain.”
– Loving my kids fiercely and still needing space from them sometimes.
– Letting go of “perfect mom” and choosing “present, honest, trying-my-best mom” instead.

My kids don’t need the version of me that’s performing motherhood to impress the internet or my own guilt. They need the version of me that’s real — the one who loves them, apologizes when she snaps, laughs with them, learns with them, and keeps showing up even on the messy days.

I don’t want to teach them to wear masks to be loved. I want to teach them, by example, that they are allowed to be fully themselves — just like their mom is learning to be.

So no, I’m not the “perfect” mom I once tried to be. I’m just me — and honestly, that’s who they needed the whole time.
