Burn It Down: When Your Old Life Doesn’t Fit Anymore

Written by Laurel

There I was — lying in bed in my grandma’s spare room, staring at the ceiling like it had all the answers I didn’t. I should’ve been happy. Everything in my life was lining up: the move, the plan, the fresh start, the “new chapter” everyone tells you is supposed to magically fix everything.

Except I wasn’t happy. I wasn’t even close.

My mind was racing, spiraling, whispering the one thing I didn’t want to hear:

If you’re getting everything you said you wanted… why are you still miserable?

When Blame Feels Easier Than Honesty

For a long time, I told myself it was everyone else’s fault.

Tyler was the reason our relationship was falling apart. Sure, I played a part — but he was the majority. I was exhausting myself trying to be the “perfect wife.” Doing everything right. Doing everything for everyone. And he didn’t appreciate any of it. So obviously, he was the problem.

The kids were wild, feral, chaotic. Again — not my fault. My mood swings didn’t help, but in my head it was Tyler undermining me, our lifestyle, our routines. I told myself I knew how to be a mother. I was trying so hard to be the perfect mom I thought I was supposed to be.

Everything was someone else’s fault. Everything was something else’s fault. And my part? Tiny. Barely worth mentioning. Because I was “trying to be perfect,” so I couldn’t possibly be the issue… right?

The Night Everything Cracked Open

But the longer I laid there in that dark room, the more uncomfortable truths started pushing their way in.

The more I replayed things, the more I realized: I didn’t actually know everything. And most of the issues in my life weren’t coming from everyone else — they were coming from me.

Not on purpose. Not because I was some kind of villain. Just… patterns I’d been living in for years without questioning them. Survival mode I’d outgrown but never updated.

Everyone else’s reactions? They weren’t the root of the problem. They were reactions to the impact I wasn’t seeing myself make.

And that was the moment everything cracked open.

I decided I wasn’t going to run from my life again. I wasn’t going to burn it all down and start over in a new place with the same old patterns.

I was going to fix me.

Burning Down the Old Version of Me

I started therapy. I started medication for my depression. I stopped blaming Tyler for everything and forced myself to remember the man I fell in love with — the man who has always loved me through every version of myself.

I stopped obsessing over our relationship like it was a problem to solve and instead… we just reconnected. Physically. Emotionally. Silly moments. Deep talks. Touch. Presence.

We brought our sex life back to life — as often as we could. Not as a “fix,” but as a way to come back home to each other. And the crazy part?

Our “problems” started to disappear. Not because we changed who we are, but because the story in my head finally did.

The truth was… there were never big unsolvable problems. There was a lot of hurt, a lot of fear, and a lot of unhealed parts of me screaming for attention.

Parenting Without the “Perfect Mom” Costume

And the kids? They started acting better, listening more, connecting deeper.

Not because they transformed overnight into calm little angels — but because I changed.

I stopped trying to parent them with some one-size-fits-all script in my head. I stopped using this invisible checklist of what a “good mom” is supposed to look like. Instead, I started meeting them where they actually are: their personalities, their moods, their needs, their energy.

I stopped chasing the Pinterest mom I thought I was supposed to be. The one who doesn’t exist. The one who would’ve made me miserable even if I’d somehow pulled it off.

The more I let go of perfection, the more I allowed myself to just be… me, the more everything made sense.

The Version of Me They Loved All Along

Tyler didn’t fall in love with some imaginary “perfect wife.” He fell in love with me — the real one.

The version of me I lost while trying to make everything look right for everyone else.

My kids don’t want the picture-perfect mom on TV. They want me — the messy, loving, emotional, goofy, growing version.

By trying so hard to be someone I wasn’t, I was accidentally teaching them to do the same: to shape-shift, to shrink, to chase impossible expectations.

Now I get to teach them something better:

Be yourself. Fully. Loudly. Imperfectly. Even while you’re still figuring out who that is.

So… What Is This Blog, Really?

This space isn’t about pretending I’ve got it all figured out. It’s not a highlight reel. It’s not a self-help manual. It’s not “ten easy steps to fix your life.”

This is my journey. My breakdowns, my rebuilds, my lessons, my chaos, my healing. My very real, very human, very imperfect life.

This is Laurel’s Organized Chaos — not the life I thought I was supposed to have, but the life I’m learning to build on purpose.

And this time?

I’m not burning it all down to run away. I’m burning down the lies, the masks, and the perfection — so I can finally build something better from the ashes.
